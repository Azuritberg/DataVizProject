Raves and Systemic Injustice

The electronic music scene has long been celebrated as a space for freedom of expression, community, and resistance against social norms. But behind the flashing lights and pulsating basslines lie structures that reflect some of society’s deeper injustices. In this article, we explore whether gender and ethnicity influence how DJs are treated within the industry, especially when it comes to who gets to perform, who earns the most, and who gains access to specific venues and collaborations.
Starting from the question “Are all DJs treated equally, or do gender and ethnicity affect how much they earn and what opportunities they get?”, we’ve divided our investigation into four key areas. Through interactive data visualizations, we examine: Who gets to work with which producers?, Who gets to perform in which cities?, Who earns the most per gig?, Who gets to perform the most? The first two graphs in this article focus on income disparities and the number of gigs, broken down by gender and ethnicity, both over time and as averages. The results reveal clear inequalities that raise questions about representation, access to opportunities, and the underlying structures that shape who gets to be heard on the dancefloor.

Is there a gender or ethnicity DJ earnings gap?

Yes, the data clearly suggests disparities in DJ earnings both by gender and ethnicity. When examining the gender-based earnings, the bar chart shows some variation in average income across gender groups, with certain genders earning slightly more than others. This difference becomes more evident in the "Over Time" line chart, where fluctuations in earnings growth trends vary significantly between the gender categories. Some gender groups experience more consistent income growth, while others display sharp drops in earnings across certain years. Similarly, the ethnicity-based earnings reveal even more pronounced differences. The bar chart indicates a clear earnings gap between the three ethnic groups, with one group earning consistently higher on average than the others. The "Over Time" chart reinforces this disparity, showing that the earnings trajectory differs not only in magnitude but also in stability across ethnic groups, some show a more stable increase, while others face volatility or stagnation. In summary, the visualizations reflect systemic inequalities in DJ income that correlate with both gender and ethnicity, underscoring the presence of an earnings gap that is likely influenced by broader structural or cultural factors within the music and events industry.

Who gets to play the most?

The data visualizations clearly indicate that access to gigs is not evenly distributed across different gender and ethnic groups. When analyzing the bar charts for total gigs, the gender group lambda and the ethnic group tau stand out with the highest number of performances. This suggests that DJs belonging to these categories consistently receive more opportunities to perform than others. The line charts further reinforce this pattern. Over time, the lambda group shows a steadily increasing trend in the number of gigs, outperforming the other gender groups. Similarly, tau not only starts strong but maintains a leading trajectory throughout the observed period, suggesting a structural advantage in access to gigs. In contrast, other groups such as omicron or psi tend to have lower and more fluctuating gig counts, reflecting potential disparities in representation or opportunity. These patterns raise important questions about equity in the music and events industry and highlight the need to examine how factors like gender and ethnicity shape who gets visibility and stage time.
